Annual Mean Air Pollution for the United States (2000 - 2016)

One major contributing factor to air pollution is fine particulate matter (PM2.5). Fine particulate matter contains microscopic droplets and solids that are 2.5 micrometers or smaller in size. Fine particulate matter is one of the air pollutants that cause smog, or the hazy, reduced visibility experienced throughout the United States. Air pollutants released by industries, power plants, and automobiles into the atmosphere create the majority of fine particulate matter. Point sources, such as construction sites, smokestacks, fires, and dust from fields and unpaved roads contribute to fine particulate matter. Fine particulate matter can be dangerous to breathe, and trigger illness, which can result in hospitalization, death. Short term exposure can seriously affect infants, children, teens, adults over the age of 65, people with chronic respiratory and cardiac issues, and people who work outdoors. Long-term exposure poses respiratory and cardiovascular harm, cancer risks and developmental harm. These fine particulates are measured in a volumetric measurement: μg/m3. Weighted Annual Mean of PM2.5 for 20 U.S. cities was measured over 16 years (2000 - 2016). The current national standard is 12 PM2.5 μg/m3 or less. PM2.5 concentrations for U.S. cities have decreased since 2000.
